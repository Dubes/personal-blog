Delivery pressure. If there is one phrase that describes the state of our industry over the last five years, it has to be this. No industry is safe. The expectations are harsh. Deliver faster. Innovate more. Deliver yesterday. More features, more things, more, more, more.

![A looping gif of Kylo Ren menancingly screaming more](https://giphy.com/embed/OsfVaOer7N2265YTRF)

How did we get here? The answer is simple enough. The expectations of investors, markets, and businesses have been rising consistenly and we have not kept up with it. 

Delivery pressure itself is not new. We used to face it every now and then — a missed deadline, an unexpected error, a misunderstood foundational requirement led to crunch time, a period where you had to put in extra hours. What _is_ different is that it is now sustained. In the past, crunch time was followed by lax time, peeks and valleys. Now, crunch time seems to have been accepted as the new norm.

# The impact

When people work under sustained stress, shortcuts are taken. Not because people are lazy or do not care, but because speed is demanded without the conditions that enable speed. Quality drops. People notice. But we still do not handle it well.

Time and again, I see the same worrying pattern. People think delivery pressure is an interim thing. If they can just push through it, work a bit harder, a bit longer, add a few more people - things will return to normal. Sadly, that is barely a strategy, let alone a good one. It is a variation of a hope and a prayer.

I feel the biggest issue is that people fundamentally misunderstand what delivery pressure is. They go by the gut. And the problem with the gut is that it is usually wrong when it comes to being agile. One of the best explanations of agile I have heard is that **agile is counterintuitive**. What you think is the right thing to do, most likely the agile way of doing it is the exact opposite. That is the beauty and the curse of agile.

# The self-fulfilling prophecy

When handled poorly, delivery pressure becomes self-fulfilling. You take more work. More rework is needed because you missed certain attributes of quality. That rework now sits on top of what you were already struggling to deliver. I have seen good teams unable to even update their dependencies because there is simply no time.

We have been dealing with this for almost half a decade. We should have become better at it by now. But we have not. Because as I said - the fix is counterintuitive.

# Stop being tactical, start being strategic

One of the best pieces of advice I received was from [Erik Dörnenburg](https://erik.doernenburg.com/): **when your plate is overflowing, you will want to focus more on the tactical stuff, but the overflowing plate is a clear signal to stop doing tactical things and start thinking strategically.** Strategy is your way out.

But before strategy, let's start with understanding. Delivery pressure is not an emotional problem. It is not a skill problem. It is not a motivation problem or a process hiccup. It is a **mathematical problem, an analytical problem — with emotional side effects**. And the best way I know to analyze it is to think in abstractions.

# The delivery company

Don't think about your setup. Find a different business that mirrors your problem.

You run a moderately successful delivery business. Letters. You have worked hard, built a reputation, earned repeat customers. Business is stable, growing. Then things fall apart. Deliveries are getting missed. Complaints are rising. Trustpilot ratings are dropping. Your workers are overworked and unhappy.

You cannot let this go. If you keep doing what you are doing without changing anything, you are entering a death spiral. So you analyze.

**What if** the city has taken up construction projects, and your normal routes now take longer? Your couriers on bicycles cannot make it in time. The fix here is good expectation setting and waiting it out. Construction projects end. Inform customers about delays, take pressure off employees. Move on.

**But what if** your customers are no longer sending letters — they are sending big parcels, sometimes even washing machines? Now the fix is completely different. The nature of your demand has changed. Maybe you need to hire more people. Maybe solo shifts need to become teams of three. Maybe you need bigger vans. Maybe fewer deliveries per person. You need to revamp your org structure and your operational setup.

The important bit: **you cannot simply double down and hope that normal service will resume.**

# Back to software

If I simplify delivery pressure in a software ecosystem, it is this: the throughput of your tickets is not matching expectations. Note — I am not saying you have _slowed down_. It might mean your scale of improvement has not kept up with the demand your business or investors have from you.

So you have to analyze what is standing in your way.

**Rework.** Has what you delivered come back? Was it bugs? Quality issues? Then your quality strategy is the place to start.

**Requirements.** Not quality, but missing requirements? Rethink how you gather them. Maybe you need more UX expertise.

**Architecture.** Architecture decisions are bets. We call them architecture decision records, but they are really predictions about what you will need. Were they right? If not, revisit them. Decisions should never be written in stone, but they also should not be trivially easy to revert. They need to be revisitable, not untouchable.

**People.** Do you have the right people playing to their strengths? Are they empowered? This is something I have seen many teams get wrong, with good intentions. They try to protect people by not burdening them with context — things not yet decided, problems that might not materialize. But your people are knowledge workers. They need information to do their job. In your goodwill to shield them, you deprive them of a vital tool. Again — counterintuitive.

**Expectation setting.** Sometimes the pressure is simply a gap between what was promised and what can realistically be delivered. Expectation setting requires vulnerability. It is a negotiation, and one party cannot dictate the other. These conversations can get heated, I will not lie. But you cannot avoid them forever. The more you avoid them, the worse you will be at them.

# Concluding thoughts

Delivery pressure is going to stay. However, that does not mean we have to simply accept it. We have to evolve and adapt to the changing demands. Analyze where the friction is. Resist the gut. Think strategically.

And honestly — ask yourself if you are the right person to lead through this change. Maybe even you are not positioned in the right place. That is a question worth asking.
