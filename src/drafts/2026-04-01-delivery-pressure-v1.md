# The new normal

Delivery pressure is not new to our industry. We have always had it. A missed deadline here, a crunch time there. What _is_ new is that delivery pressure has become sustained. In the past, crunch time was followed by recovery time. Now, the crunch _is_ the norm.

The expectations from investors, markets, and businesses have reached levels that many teams simply cannot keep up with. Deliver faster. Innovate more. Deliver yesterday. More features, more output, more, more, more.

![A looping gif of Kylo Ren menancingly screaming more](https://giphy.com/embed/OsfVaOer7N2265YTRF)

# Why we keep getting it wrong

When the pressure mounts, our gut tells us to just burn through it. Work harder, work longer, throw more people at it. If we can just survive this sprint, things will go back to normal. But they don't. That is barely a strategy, let alone a good one. It is hope and a prayer dressed up as a plan.

The problem is that people fundamentally misunderstand what delivery pressure _is_. They treat it as an emotional problem — a motivation issue, a process hiccup, something to push through. But delivery pressure is, at its core, a mathematical problem. An analytical problem. With emotional side effects.

And the fix? Counterintuitive. One of the best descriptions of agile I have heard is that it is counterintuitive. What your gut tells you to do is most likely the opposite of what you should do. That is the beauty and the curse of agile.

# The self-fulfilling prophecy

When handled poorly, delivery pressure becomes a self-fulfilling prophecy. You take shortcuts because there is no time. Those shortcuts create rework. The rework piles on top of the work you already cannot keep up with. I have seen good teams struggle to even update their dependencies because there is simply no time. We all know this cycle, and yet we have been stuck in it for the better part of five years. We should be better at this by now.

One of the best pieces of advice I received was from [Erik Dörnenburg](https://erik.doernenburg.com/): _when your plate is overflowing, you will want to focus more on the tactical stuff, but the overflowing plate is a clear signal to stop doing tactical things and start thinking strategically._ Strategy is your way out.

# Think in analogies

One of the best tools I know for breaking down a complex problem is to think in abstractions. Step away from your own setup and find a different business that mirrors your situation.

Imagine you run a moderately successful delivery business. You deliver letters. You have worked hard to build a reputation. You get repeat customers. Business is stable and growing.

Then things start falling apart. Deliveries are getting missed. Complaints are rising. Your employees are overworked and unhappy. You, as a leader, have to fix this — because doing nothing means entering a death spiral.

But the fix depends entirely on the _cause_.

**Scenario A:** The city has taken up construction projects. Your normal routes now take longer. Your couriers on bicycles cannot make it in time. In this case, the fix is expectation management and patience. Construction ends. Inform your customers about potential delays. Take some pressure off your employees.

**Scenario B:** Your customers are no longer sending letters — they are sending washing machines. The nature of the demand has fundamentally changed. Now you might need bigger vans, teams of three instead of solo couriers, fewer deliveries per shift, maybe even a pivot in your business model. You need to revamp your org structure and operations to match the new reality.

The critical insight: you cannot simply double down and hope that normal service will resume.

# Map it to software

In a software ecosystem, delivery pressure — simplified — means the throughput of your work is not matching expectations. This does not necessarily mean you have slowed down. It might mean that the scale of improvement has not kept pace with the demands placed on you.

You have to find out where the friction is. Here are some places to look:

## Is it rework?

Has a lot of what you delivered in the past come back? If so, what was the root cause? Bugs? Quality gaps? If yes, your quality strategy is the place to start.

## Is it requirements?

Was it not quality, but missing or unclear requirements? Maybe you need to rethink how you gather requirements. Maybe you need more user experience expertise.

## Is it architecture?

Architecture decisions are bets on the future. Look back at them. Were they the right bets? If not, it is time to revisit. Decisions should not be written in stone. Yes, they should not be trivially easy to revert either — but they cannot be untouchable.

## Is it people?

Do you have the right people in the right positions, playing to their strengths? Are they empowered?

This is where I have seen many teams, with good intentions, get it wrong. They try to protect people by shielding them from context — decisions not yet made, problems that might or might not land. The thinking is kind: do not burden them. But the people in your team are knowledge workers. They _need_ information. By shielding them, you are depriving them of a vital tool.

Again, counterintuitive.

## Is it expectation setting?

Sometimes the pressure is simply a gap between what was promised and what can realistically be delivered. Expectation setting requires vulnerability. It is a negotiation, and one party cannot dictate terms. These are difficult conversations, and they can get heated. But you cannot avoid them forever. The longer you do, the worse it gets.

# To conclude

Delivery pressure is here to stay. But that does not mean we have to simply accept it and burn through it. We have to analyze where the friction actually is, resist the gut reaction to just work harder, and think strategically about what needs to change.

And do not forget to ask yourself: are _you_ the right person to lead through this change? Maybe you yourself are not positioned in the right place. That question deserves honest consideration too.
